## THE RESEARCH PHASE (2:00 – 4:00)
**[Scene: Character at laptop researching salary data]**

VOICEOVER:
"Before you say a single word about money — do your homework.

**Step 1: Find your market rate.**
Use all of these:
- Glassdoor (employer-reported)
- LinkedIn Salary (network-based)
- Levels.fyi (tech-specific)
- Bureau of Labor Statistics (free, official)
- Ask people in your network

Get a range, not a number. Example: '$85,000–$105,000 for this role in this city.'

**Step 2: Know your BATNA.**
BATNA = Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement.
What's your next best option if this falls through?
Another job offer? Current job? Freelance income?

The stronger your BATNA, the more leverage you have — and the calmer you'll be.

**Step 3: Decide your numbers.**
- Target: what you actually want ($95,000)
- Minimum: lowest you'd accept ($85,000)
- Anchor: what you'll say first ($105,000)"

## THE NEW JOB NEGOTIATION (4:00 – 8:00)
**[Scene: Job offer arrives. Character picks up phone.]**

VOICEOVER:
"You get the offer. They say '$80,000.'

**Don't respond immediately.** Say this:

*'Thank you so much — I'm really excited about this opportunity. Could I have 24 to 48 hours to review the full offer?'*

Always say this. Always. Even if you plan to accept. Time lets you prepare.

**Then call back. Say this:**

*'I've reviewed the offer and I'm very enthusiastic about joining the team. Based on my research of market rates for this role, and considering my [X years of experience / specific skill / quantifiable achievement], I was expecting something closer to $95,000. Is there flexibility there?'*

Then. Stop. Talking.

This is critical. After you say the number — silence. Let them respond. The first one to speak loses negotiating power.

**If they come back at $87,000:**

*'I appreciate you working with me on this. The role is a great fit. Could we meet at $92,000? That's where I feel the compensation reflects the value I bring.'*

**If they say no to everything:**

*'I understand. Are there other elements of the package that might have flexibility — signing bonus, remote work days, additional vacation, or an earlier performance review?'*

Total compensation isn't just salary. Stock options. Bonus. Benefits. Flexibility. PTO. Professional development budget. All negotiable."

## THE RAISE NEGOTIATION (8:00 – 12:00)
**[Scene: Character walks into manager's office, prepared.]**

VOICEOVER:
"You've been in your role for 12–18 months. Time to ask for a raise.

**Step 1: Time it right.**
Best moments: after a major win, during performance review season, after a new responsibility.
Worst moments: after layoffs, during company crisis, on a Monday morning.

**Step 2: Build your case file.**
Before the meeting, document:
- Specific achievements with numbers ('increased sales by 23%', 'reduced processing time by 40%')
- New skills or certifications gained
- Market rate data showing your role pays more elsewhere
- Additional responsibilities since your last raise

**Step 3: The conversation. Say this:**

*'I'd like to talk about my compensation. I've been in this role for [X months/years], and I've taken on [list responsibilities]. Based on my contributions — specifically [achievement 1] and [achievement 2] — and market data showing comparable roles at $X, I'd like to discuss moving my salary to $95,000.'*

**If they say 'the budget is tight':**

*'I understand timing can be challenging. Would it be possible to set a formal review in 90 days with a clear path to $95,000 if I [specific measurable target]?'*

Get everything in writing. A promise without a paper trail is just a conversation."

## COMMON MISTAKES (14:00 – 15:30)
**[Scene: Red X appears over each mistake]**

VOICEOVER:
"The 5 mistakes that kill negotiations:

1. **Giving your current salary first.** Never. Say 'I'd prefer to understand the full scope of the role before discussing compensation.'

2. **Giving a range.** They'll always pick the bottom. Give one number.

3. **Apologizing for asking.** You're a professional discussing business. No apology needed.

4. **Making it emotional.** 'I need this because my rent went up.' They don't care. Make it about value, not need.

5. **Not negotiating at all.** Silence costs you more than any awkward conversation."
