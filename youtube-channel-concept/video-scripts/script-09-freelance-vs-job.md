## THE SALARY LIE (0:45 – 2:30)
**[Scene: Paycheck with hidden deductions revealed]**

VOICEOVER:
"A $65,000 salary isn't $65,000.

After federal income tax, state tax, Social Security, and Medicare — you take home about **$48,000–$52,000**.

Your employer keeps another $9,000–$10,000 in payroll taxes they never tell you about.

So a '$65,000 job' actually costs your employer $75,000 — and you see $48,000.

**The math nobody shows you:**

| | Employee (65k) | Freelancer (65k gross) |
|--|--|--|
| Gross income | $65,000 | $65,000 |
| Self-employment tax | $0 | -$9,180 |
| Business deductions | $0 | -$8,000 (est.) |
| Federal income tax | -$7,800 | -$6,200 |
| Take-home | ~$48,000 | ~$41,600 |

At the same gross income, the freelancer takes home **less** — which is why freelancers must charge 30–50% more than a salary equivalent to break even."

## THE BENEFITS CALCULATION (2:30 – 5:00)
**[Scene: List of employee benefits with dollar values attached]**

VOICEOVER:
"Employees get invisible compensation most people never count.

Let's put real numbers on it:

- **Health insurance**: Employer pays ~$7,000–$12,000/year for family coverage
- **401k match**: 3–6% match = $1,950–$3,900/year on $65k
- **PTO**: 2 weeks = $2,500 in paid time off
- **Office equipment**: $500–$2,000/year in hardware, software
- **Professional development**: $1,000–$3,000 in training/conferences

**Total hidden benefits: $13,000–$23,000/year**

Add that to your salary, and a $65,000 job is really worth **$78,000–$88,000** in total compensation.

A freelancer billing $65,000 must pay ALL of these out of pocket.

To match a $65,000 employee's total comp, a freelancer needs to bill **$95,000–$110,000/year.**

Nobody tells you that."

## WHERE FREELANCERS WIN (5:00 – 8:00)
**[Scene: Freelancer works 4-day week, travels, picks clients]**

VOICEOVER:
"But here's where freelancing reverses everything.

**Income ceiling:**
An employee's income grows at the speed of promotions — maybe 5–10% per year if you're lucky.

A freelancer's income scales with skill, reputation, and rates. There's no ceiling.

- Year 1: $30,000 (building)
- Year 2: $55,000 (growing)
- Year 3: $80,000 (established)
- Year 5: $130,000+ (high-demand specialist)

**Tax advantages:**
Freelancers can deduct:
- Home office: up to $1,500/year simplified method
- Equipment: 100% of laptops, cameras, microphones
- Software subscriptions: 100%
- Travel to client meetings: mileage + expenses
- Health insurance premiums: 100% deductible if self-employed
- Retirement: SEP-IRA allows contributions up to 25% of net income

A freelancer earning $100,000 can legally reduce taxable income to $65,000–$70,000 through deductions.

**Time autonomy:**
The average 9-to-5 worker is **productive for only 2.8 hours per day** according to productivity research. You're paid for 8.

A focused freelancer can deliver 8 hours of client value in 4 hours — then stop.

Freelancers who systematize their work often reach 30-hour work weeks at higher income than 40-hour salaried peers."

## THE RISK REALITY (8:00 – 10:30)
**[Scene: Freelancer income chart — feast and famine cycles]**

VOICEOVER:
"Now the honest part.

Freelancing is genuinely harder for the first 1–2 years.

**The income dip:**
Most new freelancers take a 20–40% income cut in year 1 as they build a client base.

You need a 6-month cash reserve before you quit your job. Non-negotiable.

**The feast/famine cycle:**
Without recurring clients, income swings wildly. One month $12,000. Next month $2,000.

The solution: retainer clients. Monthly contracts. Subscription services.

**No sick days:**
If you don't work, you don't earn. One bad month — illness, family emergency — hits your income directly.

The fix: income protection insurance ($30–$60/month) and a 3-month cash buffer at all times.

**No career ladder:**
Freelancers must self-promote, find clients, handle invoicing, and manage their own growth. Nobody promotes you.

If you're not comfortable with sales and self-marketing, freelancing is a much harder road."

## WHO SHOULD CHOOSE WHAT (10:30 – 12:30)
**[Scene: Decision matrix]**

VOICEOVER:
"The honest decision guide:

**Choose 9-to-5 if:**
- You value stability and predictability above income ceiling
- You have a family depending on consistent income and benefits
- You're early in your career and still learning the industry
- Your field doesn't lend itself to freelance (highly credentialed fields, physical presence required)

**Choose freelancing if:**
- You have a marketable skill companies will pay for (design, writing, development, marketing, consulting)
- You have 6+ months of savings before making the switch
- You're comfortable with income variability
- You want to remove the ceiling on your income
- You want location or schedule flexibility

**The hybrid path:**
Most successful freelancers didn't quit their jobs cold turkey.

They built the freelance business on nights and weekends until income hit 50–75% of their salary — then made the jump.

Target: $2,000–$3,000/month in consistent freelance income before you quit."
