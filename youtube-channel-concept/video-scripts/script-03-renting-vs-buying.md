## THE REAL COST OF BUYING (2:00 – 5:30)

**[Scene: Character buys a $400,000 house. Costs start stacking up visually]**

VOICEOVER:
"Let's say you buy a $400,000 home.

**Day one costs:**
- Down payment (20%): $80,000
- Closing costs (3%): $12,000
- Total upfront: **$92,000**

**Monthly costs:**
- Mortgage payment (30yr at 7%): $2,128/month
- Property taxes (1.2%/year): $400/month
- Home insurance: $150/month
- Maintenance (1% of value/year): $333/month

Total monthly: **$3,011**

And that's before any repairs. Roof leak? $8,000. HVAC replacement? $6,000. These are real.

**The hidden costs nobody tells you:**
Over 30 years, you'll pay $766,000 total for a $400,000 home.

That's $366,000 in interest alone — gone. Forever. To the bank.

Plus maintenance, taxes, insurance over 30 years: another $350,000+.

**Total real cost of that $400,000 home: over $1.1 million.**"

**[Scene: Character stares at the massive number in shock]**

## THE REAL COST OF RENTING (5:30 – 8:00)

**[Scene: Renter in same city pays monthly rent and invests the difference]**

VOICEOVER:
"Now let's meet the renter in the same city.

Equivalent apartment: $1,800/month in rent.

They take the $92,000 they didn't spend on a down payment and close costs, and invest it into a diversified index fund.

They also invest the monthly difference: $3,011 – $1,800 = **$1,211/month** invested.

At the historical 10% annual return of the S&P 500, over 30 years:

**The $92,000 lump sum grows to: $1.6 million.**
**The $1,211/month grows to: $2.45 million.**

**Total renter investment portfolio: $4 million+.**

Meanwhile, the homeowner sold their house after 30 years. 

If the home appreciated 3.5%/year (historical average), it's now worth $1.12 million.

After realtor fees (5%), capital gains taxes, and paying off the remaining loan balance...

**Net proceeds from home sale: ~$700,000.**"

**[Scene: Side-by-side comparison: Renter $4M vs Homeowner $700K]**

## BUT WAIT — THE HOMEOWNER'S SIDE (8:00 – 10:30)

**[Scene: Balance scale. Adding homeowner advantages one by one]**

VOICEOVER:
"Before homeowners send angry comments — I'm not done.

Here's what the math above MISSES:

**1. Rent increases. Mortgage doesn't.**
A fixed 30-year mortgage locks your payment at $2,128 forever. 
In 20 years, your rent could be $4,000/month.

**2. Leverage.**
You bought a $400,000 asset with only $80,000.
A 10% rise in home value = 50% return on your down payment.
Renters don't get leverage.

**3. Tax benefits.**
Mortgage interest is tax-deductible in many countries.
That's real savings, especially in high-income brackets.

**4. Freedom and stability.**
You can renovate, adopt a dog, paint the walls red.
Nobody can raise your rent or tell you to leave.

**5. Forced savings.**
Most people won't actually invest the difference. 
The mortgage forces wealth-building on people with poor financial discipline.

So the real answer is: it depends on YOU."

---

## WHEN RENTING WINS (10:30 – 12:00)

**[Scene: Checklist appearing on screen]**

VOICEOVER:
"Renting is the smarter financial choice when:

✅ You'll live somewhere for less than 5 years
✅ Home prices are significantly above their historical rent ratio
✅ You have the discipline to actually invest the difference
✅ You're in a high-growth career where mobility matters
✅ The local market is overpriced (price-to-rent ratio above 20)

Check your city's price-to-rent ratio: divide home price by annual rent.
Above 20? Renting likely wins financially."

---

## WHEN BUYING WINS (12:00 – 13:30)

**[Scene: Different checklist]**

VOICEOVER:
"Buying is the smarter financial choice when:

✅ You plan to stay for 7+ years
✅ The price-to-rent ratio is below 15 in your area
✅ You have a stable income and emergency fund
✅ You value stability and roots over flexibility
✅ You're honest enough to admit you won't invest the difference if you rent

The biggest mistake isn't choosing wrong.
It's making the choice based on what everyone else told you — without running your own numbers."

**[Scene: Both paths merge. Character smiles at a calculator]**

"Here's my take: the rent-vs-buy debate is a distraction from the real question.

**Are you building wealth regardless?**

A renter who invests aggressively builds wealth.
A person who buys a house they can't afford — and a renter who spends the difference on lifestyle — both lose.

Stop asking 'is renting throwing money away?'

The answer to THAT question determines your financial future."
