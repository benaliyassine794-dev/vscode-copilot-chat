## HOW LEASING WORKS (0:40 – 2:30)
**[Scene: Lease contract breakdown animated]**

VOICEOVER:
"A lease is essentially a long-term rental.

You pay for the car's depreciation during the lease period — not the whole car.

**How it's calculated:**
- Car MSRP: $35,000
- Residual value after 3 years: $21,000 (60%)
- Depreciation you pay: $14,000
- Plus: rent charge (interest), dealer fees, taxes

Your monthly payment covers depreciation + the finance company's profit.

**The catch:** You have mileage limits (usually 10,000–15,000/year). Go over? You pay $0.15–$0.25 per extra mile.

At the end of 3 years: return the car, sign a new lease, and start paying again.

You are permanently renting. You never build equity."

## HOW BUYING WORKS (2:30 – 4:00)
**[Scene: Loan amortization chart, equity building over time]**

VOICEOVER:
"When you buy with a loan, each payment builds equity.

**Example: $35,000 car, 20% down, 5-year loan at 6.5%:**
- Down payment: $7,000
- Monthly payment: $543
- Total paid over loan: $32,580 + $7,000 down = **$39,580**
- Car value after 5 years: ~$14,000

You paid $39,580 total. You have a $14,000 asset.
Net cost: **$25,580** over 5 years.

After the loan is paid: $0/month. You own the car outright.

Year 6 and 7: free transportation (except maintenance).

The person who leased has paid $18,000 (at $299/month for 6 years across 2 leases) and owns nothing."

## WHEN LEASING MAKES SENSE (7:30 – 9:30)
**[Scene: Specific use-case scenarios]**

VOICEOVER:
"Leasing isn't always wrong. There are specific situations where it's the right move.

**Situation 1: Business owner who writes off vehicle expenses**
If you use a car 100% for business and deduct the lease payments as business expenses, leasing can be more tax-efficient than owning.

**Situation 2: You must have the newest model**
Some professions — high-end real estate agents, client-facing executives — need to project an image. Leasing keeps you in a new car without depreciation loss.

**Situation 3: Short time horizon**
If you know you're moving in 2–3 years and don't want to deal with selling a car, a lease fits the timeline.

**Situation 4: Electric vehicles**
EV technology improves rapidly. Leasing an EV avoids being locked into older battery technology. And some leases allow you to pass federal EV tax credits to the lessee.

**In all other cases — buying wins.**"

## THE BEST CAR DECISION FOR WEALTH BUILDING (9:30 – 11:30)
**[Scene: Used car lot vs new car lot — value comparison]**

VOICEOVER:
"The #1 car mistake most people make isn't lease vs. buy — it's buying too much car.

A car depreciates. It is a liability, not an asset.

The wealth-building rule: **your total car expenses (payment + insurance + gas + maintenance) should not exceed 15–20% of your monthly take-home.**

On $4,000/month take-home: max $600–$800/month in total car costs.

**The wealth-builder's car strategy:**
1. Buy a used car, 2–4 years old, in cash or with a short loan (24–36 months max)
2. A 2-year-old car has already absorbed 30–40% of its depreciation — you get most of the car for 60–70% of the price
3. Drive it for 8–10 years
4. Invest the money you would have spent on car payments

**The most expensive car you can own is a new car you didn't need.**

Warren Buffett drove a used Cadillac for 8 years. He's done okay."
