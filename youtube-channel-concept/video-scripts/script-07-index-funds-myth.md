## MYTH 1: "Index Funds Are Safe" (0:35 – 2:30)
**[Scene: S&P 500 chart showing 2000, 2008, 2020 crashes — each a 30–50% drop]**

VOICEOVER:
"Myth 1: Index funds are safe.

Truth: Index funds track the market. And the market crashes. Badly.

- 2000–2002 dot-com crash: S&P 500 dropped **49%**
- 2008–2009 financial crisis: S&P 500 dropped **56%**
- March 2020: S&P 500 dropped **34%** in 33 days

If you had $100,000 in an S&P 500 index fund in 2007, by 2009 you had $44,000.

That's not safe. That's volatile.

The CORRECT statement is: index funds are safer than picking individual stocks — AND they recover and grow over long time horizons.

The dangerous version: index funds are safe, so I can invest money I'll need in 2 years.

Never invest money in index funds that you'll need in under 5 years."

## MYTH 2: "Any Index Fund Is Fine" (2:30 – 4:30)
**[Scene: Comparison table of different index funds — different fees, different returns]**

VOICEOVER:
"Myth 2: All index funds are the same.

Truth: The expense ratio — the annual fee — makes an enormous difference.

Example: You invest $10,000. Market returns 8%/year for 30 years.

| Expense Ratio | Final Value |
|--------------|-------------|
| 0.03% (VTI)  | $95,000     |
| 0.20%        | $85,000     |
| 1.00%        | $67,000     |
| 1.50%        | $57,000     |

Same investment. Same market. Difference: **$38,000** — just from fees.

Always choose funds with expense ratios below 0.20%. The best are under 0.05%:
- VTI: 0.03%
- VOO: 0.03%
- FZROX: 0.00% (literally free)"

## MYTH 3: "Buy and Forget — Never Look At It" (4:30 – 7:00)
**[Scene: Character sleeping for 30 years, wakes up to check account]**

VOICEOVER:
"Myth 3: Set it and forget it forever.

Partially true. Mostly dangerous.

The 'forget it' part is correct for day-to-day panic selling. Don't react to market news. Don't sell when it drops.

But 'forget it' applied to your overall strategy is a mistake.

Here's what you SHOULD do:

**Annual rebalancing:**
Your portfolio drifts over time. If stocks go up a lot, you might end up 95% stocks and 5% bonds when you wanted 80/20. Rebalancing once a year keeps your risk level intentional.

**Age-based adjustment:**
At 25: 90% stocks, 10% bonds.
At 40: 80% stocks, 20% bonds.
At 55: 60% stocks, 40% bonds.
At 65: 40% stocks, 60% bonds.

The closer you get to needing the money, the less volatility you can tolerate.

**Review your contribution amount:**
As your income grows, increase your monthly investment. Don't invest the same $200/month for 30 years if you're earning 3x more than when you started."

## MYTH 4: "One Index Fund Is Enough" (7:00 – 9:00)
**[Scene: Character puts all eggs in one basket. Basket falls.]**

VOICEOVER:
"Myth 4: Just buy the S&P 500 and you're done.

The S&P 500 is great. But it's 500 US companies. That's it.

The US is 60% of global stock market value. That means you're ignoring 40% of the world's companies.

In the 2000s, US stocks returned nearly 0% for a decade. International stocks returned 100%+.

Better approach:
- **70% Total US Market** (VTI or FZROX)
- **20% International** (VXUS or FZILX)
- **10% Bonds** (BND) — increases as you age

This three-fund portfolio gives you exposure to thousands of companies across every economy on earth. Diversification is free insurance."
